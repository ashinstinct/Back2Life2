Kicking Cars
Prompt + Tutorial

A first-person POV scooter ride through central London where the rider kicks a car, a double-decker bus and a black taxi cab flying out of his path with raw superhuman force — realistic crash physics, shattering glass, barrel rolls — before snatching a bag of Back2Life Energy Sweets from a Bugatti driver. One Seedance 2.0 Fast prompt, built from real reference footage of a scooter POV through London.

Tool: Seedance 2.0 Fast
Output: 1 × 9:16 video
Difficulty: Intermediate
Time: ~15 mins
Follow Instagram TikTok YouTube

What You'll Create

A single Seedance 2.0 generation built around one core trick: real POV reference footage of a scooter ride through central London. Instead of describing the streets, traffic and camera motion from scratch, the model is given actual first-person scooter footage as its reference plate — then the prompt layers superhuman action on top of it. The rider never stops moving. A car, a bus and a taxi are kicked out of the way in sequence with escalating crash physics, before the whole thing lands on a comedic beat: snatching a bag of Back2Life Energy Sweets from a Bugatti driver.

Start With Real POV Footage

The reference that makes the motion feel real

1

Shoot or source a first-person scooter clip. Handheld, chest or handlebar-mounted, riding through busy traffic. This is the single most important ingredient — Seedance reads the camera bob, the road, the passing vehicles and the daylight straight from this reference instead of you having to describe all of it in text.

2

Product reference image. A clean shot of whatever gets "stolen" in the payoff beat — for this one, a bag of Back2Life Energy Sweets. Swap this for any product when adapting the template for a client video.

3

Attach the POV clip as your primary video reference and the product shot as an image reference. The rest of the prompt tells the model what to do on top of that footage.

💡

Pro tip: Overcast daylight footage with wet or reflective road surfaces reads as more cinematic once the crash VFX are layered in — the wet tarmac catches the glass and debris beautifully.

The Full Prompt

Five parts: Style & Mood, Narrative, Dynamic, Static, Audio

1

The prompt is broken into labelled sections rather than one long paragraph. Style & Mood sets the tone in one line. Narrative Summary is the one-sentence pitch. Dynamic Description is the beat-by-beat action — this is the longest and most important section. Static Description locks the location so it doesn't drift mid-generation. Audio is written as its own block so the sound design doesn't get lost inside the action text.

2

Inside Dynamic Description, every beat follows the same shape: obstacle appears → kick lands → specific damage happens → rider keeps moving. Naming the exact damage each time (dented panel, shattered rear glass, barrel roll, shopfront smash) stops the model from repeating the same generic hit.

3

The word "never stopping" and "never breaks pace" appear twice in the prompt on purpose — POV action clips drift toward slowing down or cutting away, and repeating the instruction keeps the momentum honest across the full generation.

📋 Kicking Cars — Full Prompt
Style & Mood: Fast-paced action-comedy, photorealistic first-person POV scooter ride through central London, constant forward momentum, ultra-realistic vehicle destruction with natural crash physics, overcast English daylight, handheld camera.

Narrative Summary: A scooter rider moves at constant speed through dense central London traffic, kicking a car, a red double-decker bus, and a black taxi cab out of his path with raw superhuman force — each kick sending vehicles flying with realistic crash physics — then snatches a bag of Back2Life Energy Sweets from a Bugatti driver and holds it up to the camera.

Dynamic Description: First-person POV from the scooter, constant forward motion, never stopping. The rider flows through dense London traffic with vehicles to his left and right at all times. A car pulls across his path — without slowing, he kicks the back of it, the rear panel caves in with a massive dent, the rear windows shatter and glass crumbles down as the car is shoved sideways out of his way. He keeps moving. A red double-decker bus drifts into his lane — he kicks it and the bus is shoved across the road, smashing into a cluster of parked cars on the opposite side with realistic crumpling metal and debris. A black taxi cab cuts across in front of him — he kicks it hard and the cab tumbles into a sideways barrel roll over two cars, crashing onto the pavement and smashing through a shopfront window with realistic crash physics. He never breaks pace, continuing to weave through the traffic. He pulls alongside a sleek black Bugatti with neon underglow — the driver is eating a bag of Back2Life Energy Sweets. The rider reaches out, grabs the bag from the driver's hand, holds it up to the camera with a grin, and rides off.

Static Description: Central London street near Westminster — Big Ben and the Houses of Parliament in the background, pavements busy with tourists and English pedestrians, red double-decker buses and black taxi cabs throughout, grey overcast sky, wet road reflections, shopfronts lining the streets.

Audio: Constant engine hum from the scooter, surrounding London traffic — horns honking, bus engines rumbling. Each kick lands with a heavy metallic thud followed by the sound of metal crumpling, glass shattering, tyres screeching on tarmac. The taxi barrel roll brings a cascade of crashing metal and breaking glass. Pedestrians shout in shock. A satisfied comedic beat when the Back2Life Energy Sweets bag is snatched.
🎯

Why three vehicles? One kick reads as a fluke, two reads as a pattern, three with escalating damage (dent → shove → full barrel roll through a shopfront) reads as a build. The Bugatti beat then resets the tone from destruction to comedy in one line.

4

Adapting the Template

Swap the product, the city, the vehicles

1

Product swap. Replace "Back2Life Energy Sweets" with any client product for a UGC-style action ad — the five-part structure doesn't need to change, only the payoff line and the product reference image.

2

City / landmark swap. Replace the Static Description with a different city's landmarks and vehicle types (yellow cabs and an NYPD cruiser for New York, tuk-tuks for Bangkok) — keep the same POV reference clip technique, just source or shoot footage from that city instead.

3

Vehicle swap. Any obstacle works as long as each one gets a distinct, named piece of damage. Motorbikes, delivery vans, market stalls — the escalation pattern (dent → shove → barrel roll) is what sells it, not the specific vehicle.

💡

Pro tip: Keep this prompt saved in a viral-prompts library. Once one version performs well, re-running it with a new city or product is a five-minute edit, not a from-scratch rebuild.
